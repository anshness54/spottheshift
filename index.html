The Science

Urban Evolution — The Biology

Two things you need to understand to make sense of everything SpotTheShift tracks: what evolution actually is, and how cities supercharge it.

On this page
What is Evolution? Urban Heat Island Chemical & Structural Pressure
Topic 1

What Is Evolution?

Evolution is simply change in a population over time. Not in one individual — populations don't evolve, individuals do. Evolution means that the average traits of an entire group shift from one generation to the next because some individuals survive and reproduce better than others.

Here's the core idea: every living thing inherits DNA from its parents, and that DNA is never copied perfectly. Small random errors — mutations — create variation. Some individuals end up slightly better suited to their environment than others. Those individuals live longer, have more offspring, and pass on their traits. Over generations, the population gradually looks more and more like its most successful members.

A simple example in the South: Imagine a population of Green Anoles living in a Georgia city. A few have a mutation that makes their toe pads slightly larger. On slippery glass windows and metal siding, these anoles can run faster, escaping predators and catching more bugs. They survive longer and have more babies. Their offspring inherit the larger toe pads. After 20 generations, most anoles in the city have large toe pads. The population evolved.

The four things evolution needs

Variation

Individuals must differ from each other. Without variation there's nothing to select between. Mutations constantly generate new combinations.

Heritability

Traits must be passed from parent to offspring. If advantageous traits weren't inherited, selection couldn't accumulate them.

Selection pressure

Something in the environment must make some traits more advantageous than others — predators, heat, toxins, or traffic.

Time (generations)

Change accumulates slowly. Species with fast generation times (insects, small fish, mice) can visibly evolve in decades.

Evolution is not "trying to adapt"

Evolution doesn't plan ahead. Organisms don't consciously change to fit their environment. Mutations happen randomly; selection is blind; what survives is just whatever happened to work in this environment, right now.

Topic 2

The Urban Heat Island Effect

Spend an afternoon in downtown Atlanta or Savannah, then drive 30 minutes into the countryside. On a still summer evening, you'll physically feel the difference — the city is noticeably warmer. This is the Urban Heat Island (UHI) effect, and it's one of the clearest ways cities create the selection pressure that drives rapid evolution.

Why cities are hotter

Three things work together to trap heat in cities. First, dark surfaces like asphalt absorb far more solar radiation than forest canopy. Second, no cooling from plants: vegetation releases water vapor that cools the surrounding air. Paving it over removes that cooling effect entirely. Third, waste heat from human activity (car engines, air conditioners) dumps thermal energy directly into the urban atmosphere.

By the numbers: Cities in the American South, like Atlanta, Dallas, and Memphis, regularly see summer nighttime temperatures 3–5°C hotter than surrounding rural counties. Extreme sites can spike up to +8°C hotter.

How heat creates evolutionary pressure

A temperature difference of 3–5°C rewrites the rules of survival for wildlife:

Insects hit thermal maximums. The Acorn Ant, found across the Eastern US and Georgia, lives inside small acorns. On urban asphalt, those acorns bake. Ants that cannot tolerate temperatures of 43°C (109°F) simply die. Evolution has rapidly favored ants with higher Critical Thermal Maximums (CTmax).

Heat also advances the emergence timing of insects, decoupling them from their bird predators, and eliminates urban frost, which caused urban White Clover to genetically stop producing its protective cyanide (HCN) defense.

Topic 3

Chemical & Structural Pressures

Beyond temperature, human cities introduce novel chemicals and entirely new structural environments that have never existed in evolutionary history. When organisms can't flee these environments, they either die out or evolve at blinding speed.

Toxic Selection: The Atlantic Killifish

Along the US East Coast (from Virginia to New Jersey), industrial sites dumped lethal levels of polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs) and dioxins into estuaries. These chemicals cause devastating heart deformities in fish embryos. But in heavily polluted areas like the Elizabeth River, the Atlantic Killifish survived. They evolved a genetic mutation that essentially "turns off" the AHR signaling pathway. This mutation makes them up to 8,000 times more resistant to PCBs than unmutated fish. It is one of the most extreme cases of rapid vertebrate evolution ever recorded, directly driven by human pollution.

Predation Relaxation & Thermoregulation: The Gray Squirrel

In rural areas, Eastern Gray Squirrels with a mutation for black fur (melanism) stick out like sore thumbs to hawks and hunters. But in urban parks, hunting is banned, and natural predators are rare. Furthermore, in northern US cities, black fur absorbs more solar radiation, helping squirrels survive cold winters on frozen concrete. Without the heavy penalty of predation, the melanic allele is rapidly increasing in urban squirrel populations across the United States, reaching 40% or more in some city centers.
